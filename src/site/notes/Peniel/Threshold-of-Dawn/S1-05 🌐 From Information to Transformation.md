# From Information to Transformation

## When Knowing Becomes Being

There's a saying in Christian tradition that the longest journey you'll ever take is eighteen inches — from your head to your heart. Most of us nod when we hear that. We agree. And the eighteen inches remain uncrossed.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: **you can know everything about God and still not be changed by it.**

Paul said it plainly: "I do not do what I want to do, but I do what I hate" (Romans 7:15). He wasn't describing ignorance. He knew the right thing. The information was complete. What was missing was the mechanism that turns knowing into *being*.

## The Word That Passes Through You

In our last session, we discovered that the Word of God is alive — biologically, actively alive. But aliveness isn't enough. A surgeon's blade is alive with purpose only when it penetrates.

Hebrews 4:12 doesn't stop at "alive and active." It continues: the Word *"penetrates even to dividing soul and spirit, joints and marrow."*

That word "penetrates" in Greek is *diikneómai* — it means to pass completely through something. Not surface contact. Not a glancing blow. The Word moves through the entire person, reaching places your intellect can't access on its own.

And then it does something extraordinary: it **divides**.

## Soul vs. Spirit: The Cut You Need

The Writer of Hebrews says the Word divides *psychē* (soul) from *pneuma* (spirit). Most of us have these fused together. We can't tell the difference between an emotional response and a spiritual encounter. We confuse intellectual agreement with genuine transformation.

Think about it this way:

When you say "I love God," part of that is *psychē* — emotional attachment, comfort, cultural identity. And part of it is *pneuma* — genuine surrender, obedience even when it costs everything, encounter with the Holy.

When you say "I know the Bible," part of that is *psychē* — information storage, the ability to win an argument. And part of it is *pneuma* — being known *by* the Word, willingness to be undone by what you read.

**These are not the same thing.** And until the Word makes the cut — until it divides what you thought was one thing into two — you can't see which parts of your faith are real and which are performance.

That sounds scary. But here's the gift: **what remains after the cut is true.** The pneuma that survives the sword's division isn't less than what you had. It's *more*, because it's genuine.

## Your Body Already Knows

Here's why the Writer adds "joints and marrow" after "soul and spirit." It seems redundant — unless you understand that transformation is embodied.

Joints are where actions hinge. Marrow is where blood is made — where life is manufactured at the cellular level. The Word doesn't just change your theology. It changes your body. It reaches the connection points where your choices pivot and the deep core where your life-force is generated.

This is why you can know something for years and then one day — in a moment of vulnerability or crisis — it suddenly becomes *real*. The information didn't change. You changed. The Word finally penetrated from psychē to pneuma, from joints to marrow.

Think of the moment in *Ratatouille* when the food critic Anton Ego takes one bite and is transported back to his mother's kitchen. Decades of intellectual expertise about food collapse into a single, wordless experience. His analysis gives way to encounter. He's not the same person who walked through the door.

That's what the Word does when it penetrates. Not more information. Transformation.

## The Blade at the Deep Level: Sod and Dopamine

In the PaRDeS framework — the four layers of reading Scripture — this session reaches the deepest level: **Sod** (secret, hidden). S1-01 was Peshat (surface — waking up). S1-02 was Remez (patterns — seeing the walls). S1-03 was Derash (emotional — the threshold crossing). S1-04 revealed the blade's anatomy. Now Sod asks: what is happening beneath your awareness, at the myelōn level where neural pathways form and habits become identity?

The Hebrew word for this session is **avar** (עָבַר) — to cross over, to pass through. It's the root of the word "Hebrew" itself: *ivri* means "one who crosses over." Abraham was called Hebrew because he crossed from the known world into covenant. The Word of God does the same thing in you — it doesn't stop at the surface. It *avars* through you, from psychē to pneuma.

The neurochemistry that matters here is **dopamine** — and not in the way you'd expect. Dopamine is the reward chemical, and *information-collecting is a dopamine loop*. Every new insight, every new theological framework, every new book gives you a hit. The dopamine says: *more, learn more, know more*. This is why Bible study groups can meet for twenty years and never transform. The dopamine of knowing *about* God substitutes for the costly vulnerability of being known *by* God.

The shift from information to transformation is a shift from dopamine to depth. The Sod level activates not when you add knowledge but when you subtract the barriers keeping existing knowledge from penetrating.

## The Counter-Intuitive Truth

Here's what this session reveals: **you may already know everything you need to know.**

The problem isn't that you need more Bible study, more sermons, more books. The problem is that the truth you already possess hasn't made the eighteen-inch journey. It lives in your prefrontal cortex but hasn't reached your limbic system, your autonomic nervous system, your joints and marrow.

The counter-intuitive theme: *Abundance Through Scarcity*. The path to transformation isn't adding more knowledge. It's subtracting the barriers that keep existing knowledge from becoming embodied. Fewer books, deeper encounter. Stop collecting truth and start letting truth collect you.

What scarcity develops, counter-intuitively, is integration. When you stop accumulating knowledge and sit with what you already have, the left hemisphere's analytical processing begins to connect with the right hemisphere's embodied, felt experience. The result isn't just sharper thinking — it's a more whole way of knowing. Researchers call this *felt sense*: the body-knowing that lives in the gut, not the head.

The ancient Hebrews understood this intuitively. Among all the body's organs, the kidneys were considered the seat of deepest knowing — the most spiritually significant part of the body. Modern anatomy offers a quiet confirmation: the gut's enteric nervous system contains roughly 100 million neurons and produces more serotonin than the brain. What the Hebrews called wisdom in the inward parts, we now recognize as the enteric nervous system. The ancient intuition and modern neuroscience are describing the same phenomenon: truth settles in the body before it fully arrives in the mind.

## This Week's Practice: One Truth, One Body

Choose one truth you already know — "God loves me," "I am forgiven," "Rest is good." Each day:

**Say it** aloud. Notice if it feels like information or encounter.

**Locate it** in your body. Where does it live — head, chest, gut, or nowhere at all?

**Do one thing** that embodies it. Not a grand gesture. A micro-action. If you know God loves you, accept help without apologizing. If you know rest is sacred, take five minutes of genuine stillness without your phone.

**Notice** at the end of the day: did the truth feel different after the action? More real? Less abstract?

Three minutes of focused practice plus one embodied action per day. That's all it takes to begin the migration from psychē to pneuma.

If you notice the truth "living" higher in your body at the start of the week and lower by the end — descending from head toward chest or gut — that's the Word doing its work. Your body is registering transformation before your mind can name it.
